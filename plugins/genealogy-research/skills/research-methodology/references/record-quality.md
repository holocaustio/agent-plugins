# Record Quality Realities

These are 19th and early 20th century records, often transcribed multiple times. Understanding their limitations is essential for accurate research.

## Missing Records

- Not all births, marriages, and deaths were registered
- Entire years may be absent from a database
- A person may have 10 children but only 7 appear in birth records
- Wars, fires, and the Holocaust destroyed many original registers
- Some regions have excellent coverage; others have almost nothing

**The rule:** Never assume a record doesn't exist just because one database doesn't have it. Try multiple databases, multiple spelling variants, and multiple match types before concluding something is missing.

## Spelling Chaos

The same surname may appear 5+ ways across records:
- Clerks wrote what they heard, in whichever language they used (German, Czech, Hungarian, Hebrew)
- Example: SZENITZ / SENITZ / SPENITZ / PISK — all the same family
- Phonetic matching (`Q`) catches many variants; also try soundex (`D`) and fuzzy (`F1`/`F2`)

## Age Approximations

- Ages on death and marriage records are often off by 1-3 years
- A death record saying "age 79" and a birth record implying age 82 may be the same person
- Don't reject a match solely because of a 2-3 year age discrepancy
- Cross-reference with other records to determine the most likely birth year

## Missing Parents

- Many records list only the father
- Mother's name may be absent entirely
- Mother may appear under maiden name in one record and married name in another
- Patronymic names (e.g., "Mosesson") may replace the family surname
- Place-derived names may substitute for family surnames

## Language Shifts

The same town and person appear in German, Czech, Hungarian, and Hebrew across different records:
- Towns: Mikulov = Nikolsburg, Güssing = Németújvár, Wien = Bécs
- Names: Hebrew → Yiddish → German → Hungarian variants
- Example: Devorah → Dobresch → Leopoldine (same woman)
- Example: Israel → Izsak (Hebrew → Hungarian)
- Example: Meier = Max = Mayer = Markus (multiple German forms)

## Mother's Surname Chaos

The same woman may be recorded under different names in each child's birth record:
- Maiden name (most common)
- Married name
- Patronymic
- Place-derived name

This is normal and expected — don't assume different mothers just because the surname differs.

## Destroyed Records

- The Holocaust destroyed not just people but also their paper trail
- Community registers, synagogue records, and civil records were often burned
- Some regions (Vienna, Prague) have relatively good survival; others (small towns in Poland, Ukraine) have almost nothing
- Post-war reconstructions exist but are incomplete
- Yad Vashem and other memorial projects have partial reconstructions
